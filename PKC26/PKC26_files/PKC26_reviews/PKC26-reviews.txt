Review #248A
===========================================================================

Paper summary
-------------
The paper introduces and proposes the construction of a Committee Key Scheme (CKS). The CKS is built from aggregatable signature scheme, and a SNARK. Two specific SNARKs are constructed, a basic construction which uses one field element for one bit, and a packed construction which uses a field element for a fixed length bit string.

The motivation for CKS arises from the need to implement light client verifiers which work as a bridge between two blockchains. The paper proposes a construction of a light client verifier based on the CKS.

All the constructions have been implemented, and some timing results of the implementations are described.

Novelty, methodology and technical correctness
----------------------------------------------
The paper presents an extensive coverage of the background material, adequately place the contributions of the paper within the existing works.

Technical quality
-----------------
The main problem with the paper is that it is too long. Much of the technical material is in the appendix. Long papers typically place proofs in the appendix. However, the present paper also places the constructions in the appendix.

Some comments are given below.

1. Section 3 (the material on implementation) should typically come after the description of the schemes. Placing this material before the schemes is peculiar and causes problems. For example, the meanings of "basic accountable" and "packed accountable" in Tables 2 and 3 become clear only in the first paragraph of Section 5.
2. Page 15, Line 7 of Section 4. The full stop between negl(\lambda) and poly(\lambda) looks like a multiplication sign. Please rephrase to avoid confusion.
3. By the randomness r, you probably mean that r is a random non-empty finite length bit string. Please clarify.
4. In the definition of perfect completeness for aggregation on Page 17, you do not specify computational restrictions on the adversary \mathcal{A}.
5. The one paragraph description of Instantiation 1 on Page 17 is not sufficient to understand the scheme.
6. I did not notice any prior Theorem/Lemma/Proposition to Corollary 1. Generally corollaries are follow up result to some prior result.
7. The instantiations of the basic accountable protocol on Pages 22 and 23 is crammed into a single paragraph. It is hard to make out the idea.

One suggestion would be to rewrite the paper where the basic accountable scheme and the related schemes are described in the main text in a clear manner. The packed accountable scheme may be placed in the appendix.

Editorial quality
-----------------
The writing is generally clear. At places there are long sentences (examples are first paragraph on Page 8, first paragraph of Section 4.3, first paragraph on Page 23) which are difficult to parse. It will be helpful to the readers, if such long sentences are rewritten into a number of short sentences.

Scientific quality
------------------
The problem tackled in the paper is interesting. Also, the construction ideas appear to be interesting. As mentioned above, the main problem is that much of the construction is in the appendix. Since the published version will have 30 pages, the constructions will not appear in the published version. This is undesirable. I note the following sentence which appears on the submission page (https://pkc.iacr.org/2026/papersubmission.php).

"The reviewers are not required to read the auxiliary supporting material and submissions should be intelligible without it."

I doubt whether that the novelty of the paper can be judged without reading the material in the appendix.

Review #248B
===========================================================================

Paper summary
-------------
This paper addresses a challenge in blockchain interoperability by introducing an accountable light client system for proof-of-stake blockchains. The authors present a novel committee key scheme (CKS) that enables efficient verification of validator signatures using commitments to public keys rather than transmitting complete key lists. The scheme produces succinct proofs demonstrating that a threshold subset of validators signed a message, using a bit vector to identify signers while maintaining accountability.

The technical contribution consists of two custom SNARKs (basic accountable and packed accountable) optimized for fast proving times. The system enables on-chain light client verifiers on resource-constrained blockchains to validate consensus from other chains with O(1) proof size plus bitvector length, representing approximately 100× reduction in communication overhead compared to transmitting full public keys. The work includes formal security proofs, a two-step PLONK-inspired compiler for hybrid SNARKs, and a complete prototype implementation demonstrating real-world feasibility for secure blockchain bridges.

Novelty, methodology and technical correctness
----------------------------------------------
The paper generally covers the foundational literature well but misses some critical recent developments in the field of zero-knowledge bridges and folding schemes.
For example, The paper does not sufficiently discuss zkBridge (Wang et al., CCS 2022) and its use of the deVirgo proof system. zkBridge is a direct competitor that solves the same problem (fast light client proofs) using parallelization rather than the Committee Key Scheme aggregation method. A comparison of prover times and communication overhead is missing.
Also, since the paper introduces a custom "Hybrid SNARK" to handle iterative updates (validator set changes), it should discuss Folding Schemes like Nova (Microsoft Research, CRYPTO 2023). Folding schemes are currently the state-of-the-art for incrementally verifiable computation (e.g., verifying a chain of headers), and they offer a potentially more efficient alternative to the monolithic SNARK approach presented here.

The authors' methodology is rigorous, appropriate, and well-executed. Improvements are possible. While the authors measure prover time and proof size extensively, the methodology lacks a concrete analysis of on-chain verification costs (Gas). For a bridge paper, the cost to verify the proof on a target chain (e.g., Ethereum EVM) is a critical metric that is discussed only abstractly. If I understood correctly though, Ethereum is not the main target chain for the authors.

Last but not least, the claim that the authors' approach can be deployed "without compromise" is slightly overstated. As noted in the paper, chains using Ed25519 (like Cosmos/Solana) cannot use this efficiently without a hard fork to support BLS or expensive non-native field arithmetic. This is a practical barrier, though not a theoretical flaw.

Technical quality
-----------------
Nothing specific to report here.

Editorial quality
-----------------
The paper is decently-structured and clearly written overall. Improvements are possible.
Indeed,  the extensive appendices (A through N) are valuable but make the full document challenging to navigate. Consider adding a visual roadmap figure showing how the different technical components (AS, CKS, SNARKs, light client) interconnect.

Scientific quality
------------------
This line of research is certainly important in the context of blockchain interoperability.
Though things are evolving pretty fast and schemes that looked promising are quickly surpassed by other schemes that leverage other tricks. What will make the difference here is impact that in practice these schemes will have.

Review #248C
===========================================================================

Paper summary
-------------
The paper studies how to design efficient and accountable light clients for PoS blockchains. In PoS systems such as Polkadot or Ethereum, a verifier (or bridge) must be convinced that a sufficiently large quorum of validators signed a block. Sending all validator public keys or maintaining a full on-chain key list makes proofs very large.

To address this, the authors propose a committee key scheme (CKS): they commit to the entire validator key set using a polynomial commitment (a short “committee key”), and then use a SNARK to prove that a bitvector-selected subset of validators indeed aggregates to a given public key that verifies a BLS aggregate signature on the message. Thus the verifier only needs the committee key, the bitvector, and a constant-size SNARK proof, instead of the full list of keys.

Building on CKS, the paper constructs an accountable light client: if a light client is ever convinced of a state that conflicts with the finalized state on the home chain, then (under suitable assumptions on the underlying consensus) one can algorithmically extract a set of misbehaving validators whose signatures support the conflicting proofs, enabling slashing.


Strengths:

The committee key scheme gives a clean abstraction for proving that a large subset of a registered validator set signed a message, using a single commitment and a bitvector. It leverages polynomial commitments and SNARKs to reduce proof size by roughly two orders of magnitude compared to naively listing public keys.

The paper formalizes and instantiates an accountable light client: if the light client accepts a false proof, the resulting transcript can be turned into verifiable evidence against a substantial set of validators. This is highly relevant for PoS bridges where slashing is the main deterrent.

Weaknesses:

The CKS compresses public keys but still requires an n-bit bitvector, where n is the validator count. For n in the 10⁵ range, this is on the order of tens of kilobytes per proof. The paper acknowledges that this is much smaller than sending all keys, but it does not evaluate the practical gas/storage impact of repeatedly handling such bitvectors on a target chain. A more concrete discussion of what values of n remain viable in practice, and an explicit cost analysis for at least one realistic deployment scenario, would strengthen the work.

Novelty, methodology and technical correctness
----------------------------------------------
Q1. Related work completeness.
I remain concerned that the related-work section is not up to date: the references effectively stop around 2022. It would be helpful for the authors to explicitly discuss whether there are newer results on accountable or succinct light clients, commit-and-prove SNARK frameworks, or related accountability notions, and to position their contributions against that recent literature.

Q2. Methodology.
Yes. Given the goals, the chosen methodology—defining a generic CKS abstraction, instantiating it via BLS + polynomial commitments + custom SNARKs, and then plugging it into an accountable light client framework—is appropriate.

Q3. Technical correctness.
I did not identify a concrete soundness flaw in the core construction. However, many details are delegated to the appendices, and the current main/appendix mismatch (inconsistent numbering and references) makes it harder than necessary to systematically check all proofs. From a reviewer’s perspective, this weakens confidence in the presentation, even if the underlying ideas appear sound.

Technical quality
-----------------
Q4. Are there technical details missing?
The necessary details mostly exist, but they are spread across long appendices whose versioning is not fully synchronized with the main text. The heavy reliance on appendices is acceptable for a theory-leaning paper, but the authors should:
(i) ensure that all cross-references (to definitions, lemmas, instantiations) are consistent between main and appendix;
(ii) surface the key security notions (especially for accountability) more prominently in the main body;
(iii) and provide a concise “roadmap” explaining how the various SNARK compiler steps and assumptions fit together.

Editorial quality
-----------------
Q5: The writing should be significantly improved, and important results should be included in the body. In addition:

1) Placement of core definitions.
Accountability is a central contribution, yet the precise definitions of accountability completeness and accountability soundness are still buried in the appendices. For readers (and implementers), it would be much clearer if the main text contained at least the informal but precise statements of these properties, with the fully formal games in the appendix.

2) Related work appears outdated.
The related-work section still does not cite results beyond roughly 2022, even though there has been continued progress on succinct proofs, commit-and-prove SNARKs, and accountability-style notions (e.g., group-signature traceability, traitor tracing, accountable CRS generation, and newer light-client/bridge designs). As it stands, the paper risks under-positioning itself relative to 2023–2025 developments. A more up-to-date survey is needed to convincingly argue novelty.

3) Versioning and numbering inconsistencies.
The current submission clearly looks like a "stitched-together" combination of a newer main body with an older version of the appendices and bibliography. As a result, there are many inconsistencies: reference numbers in the appendix do not match the bibliography in the main file; instantiations and definitions are numbered differently in main vs. appendix; and several cross-references (e.g., to “definition C in Section 5”, “instantiation A/7”) no longer point to the correct objects. This makes it unnecessarily hard to follow and verify the technical arguments. Moreover, some of these presentational issues (moving key definitions into the main text, cleaning up references) had already been highlighted in earlier feedback but have not been fully addressed in the current revision.

Scientific quality
------------------
Q6. Importance of the research question.
The research question is important. Secure cross-chain interoperability and bridge design are central topics in today’s blockchain ecosystem. As validator sets grow into the tens or hundreds of thousands, naive light-client verification becomes impractical. A construction that offers succinct, accountable light-client proofs directly addresses a pressing need.

Q7. Scientific contribution.
The contributions are meaningful:

The CKS primitive is conceptually clean and may be reusable wherever one needs succinct evidence that a large subset signed a message.

The framework for accountable light clients, together with a concrete SNARK-based instantiation, is valuable for the broader PoS and bridging community.

At the same time, the perceived strength of the contribution is currently diminished by the outdated related-work discussion and by the “stitched-together” editing issues, which make the paper feel less polished than the underlying ideas deserve.
